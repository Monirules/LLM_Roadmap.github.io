Learning Roadmap

From Classical ML to LLM, MLLM & VLM

This website presents a structured 7-week learning roadmap for moving from classical Machine Learning toward modern NLP, Large Language Models, Multimodal LLMs, and Vision-Language Models. It is designed for learners who already know basic ML and want to prepare for PhD-level research in NLP and LLM-related areas.

  • Step-by-step weekly learning plan from PyTorch basics to advanced LLM and VLM research topics.
  • Includes theory, coding practice, research papers, mini-projects, and weekly milestones.
  • Covers Transformers, Hugging Face, BERT, GPT, LoRA, QLoRA, RAG, CLIP, LLaVA, and multimodal models.
  • Provides progress tracking, must-read papers, reading resources, project ideas, compute guidance, and a daily study schedule.

Projects

Research-Style Mini-Projects

Intermediate

Project 1: Domain-Adaptive Fine-tuning Comparison

Take domain-specific data (medical, legal, scientific). Compare: (a) BERT fine-tuned on in-domain data, (b) LLM with few-shot prompting, (c) LoRA-adapted LLM. Evaluate all three. Write a 2-page report with error analysis.

Skills: Experimental comparison, ablation analysis, scientific writing.

Intermediate

Project 2: LLM Hallucination Detection on VQA

Run LLaVA or BLIP-2 on a VQA dataset. Systematically identify hallucinations — categorize by type (object, attribute, relation). Propose detection heuristics. Very active research area.

Skills: Error analysis, taxonomy creation, identifying research gaps.

Advanced

Project 3: RAG vs Fine-tuning for Knowledge-Intensive QA

Build QA on a specialized knowledge base. Compare: (a) fine-tuned model, (b) RAG with retriever + LLM, (c) prompted LLM. Evaluate factual accuracy, hallucination rate, latency.

Skills: System design, multi-component evaluation, trade-off analysis.

Advanced

Project 4: Cross-lingual Transfer with Multilingual LLMs

Test mBERT, XLM-R, or multilingual LLM on English, then zero-shot transfer to Bengali or Swahili. Analyze where transfer fails. Relevant if lab works on multilingual NLP.

Skills: Cross-lingual evaluation, linguistic analysis, model limitations.

Intermediate

Project 5: Prompt Engineering Benchmark

Take open-source LLM + reasoning benchmark (GSM8K or StrategyQA). Compare zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought, self-consistency. Include cost/token analysis.

Skills: Systematic evaluation, statistical analysis, reproducible experiments.

Reading Resources

Free Textbooks & Papers

📚 Core Textbooks

TextbookW1–W7

Deep Learning — Goodfellow, Bengio & Courville (MIT Press)

The foundational DL textbook. Free HTML version. Read in order: Ch 6 (Feedforward Networks → W1), Ch 10 (Sequence Modeling → W2), Ch 9 (CNNs, skim → W6). Part I (Ch 2–5) for math refresher if needed. Don't read cover-to-cover — use as reference alongside lectures.

Key chapters: 6 · 9 · 10 · 11 · 12 · Part I for math review
TextbookW2–W7

Speech and Language Processing, 3rd Ed. Draft — Jurafsky & Martin (Stanford)

The NLP bible. Free PDF chapters updated Jan 2026. This is your primary NLP theory textbook. Read: Ch 3 (N-grams → W2), Ch 6 (Vector Semantics → W2), Ch 7 (Neural Nets & LMs → W1–W2), Ch 8 (Transformers → W3), Ch 9 (Pretraining: BERT, GPT → W3–W4), Ch 11 (Fine-Tuning → W4–W5), Ch 12 (LLMs → W5).

Key chapters: 3 · 6 · 7 · 8 · 9 · 11 · 12 — also available as single PDF
TextbookW2–W3

Natural Language Processing — Jacob Eisenstein (Georgia Tech / MIT Press)

Free PDF on GitHub. More formal/mathematical than Jurafsky — excellent for understanding the ML foundations of NLP. Read Ch 1–4 (classification, word embeddings) and Ch 6–7 (sequences, neural models) alongside CS224N lectures in Weeks 2–3.

Key chapters: 1–4 (fundamentals) · 6–7 (sequences) · Free PDF under CC license

Daily Schedule

5–6 hours on weekdays, 6–8 on weekends. Consistency beats intensity — six focused hours every day beats twelve some days and zero others.

Session 1
9:00–11:00 AM — Theory & Lectures (2 hrs). Watch lectures, read papers. Pen and paper. Pause and think.
Break
11:00–11:30 — Walk, rest. No screens.
Session 2
11:30–1:30 PM — Coding Practice (2 hrs). Implement what you learned. Code from scratch first, then compare with references.
Break
1:30–3:00 — Lunch and full break.
Session 3
3:00–5:00 PM — Paper Reading + Review (2 hrs). One paper/blog post. Consolidate notes. Skim HF Daily Papers.
Evening
Optional — Light review. Skim code from the day. Write 3-sentence summary. No new topics at night.
On Pace
Don't try to understand everything perfectly before moving on. If stuck 3 days on one derivation, you're going too deep. Mark it, move on, revisit during Week 7.

Final Advice

Keep a "questions document" throughout these 7 weeks. Every confusion or curiosity, write it down. By Week 7, it contains seeds of your research interests. The best PhD projects start as "I noticed something weird and wanted to understand why." Your confusion today is your research intuition tomorrow.
